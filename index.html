Stereophile Style: In General

General Style Notes on Technical Matters

Frequency Response/Range: When specifying frequency response, remember that response requires a tolerance; ie, frequency response 20Hz–20kHz, ±3dB. Without any tolerance quoted, it becomes a range: 20Hz–20kHz.

Signal/Noise Ratio: Use signal/noise and S/N ratio, rather than signal-to-noise. By definition, a ratio must be positive: a S/N ratio of 100dB is correct, but a S/N ratio of –100dB is wrong.

Stereo Separation: Separation is positive, crosstalk is negative: a separation of 50dB is the same as a crosstalk of –50dB.

Loudspeaker Sensitivity: This is specified awkwardly as dB/W/m. I say awkwardly because we are talking about a sound-pressure level out for an applied voltage, not power (pressure and voltage are analogous). Convention, and a hangover from the obsolete term efficiency, however, dictate that power be used. As far as I can tell, manufacturers interpret a “watt” as 2.83V into an 8 ohm load, and it is this 2.83V that is referred to in sensitivity specifications. If reviewing a loudspeaker that has a nominal impedance very much less than 8 ohms, this must be pointed out, as the 2.83V will be equivalent to more than 1W. The Apogee Scintilla’s 1 ohm impedance, for example, means that its sensitivity is artificially improved by the fact that it draws 8W from the amplifier for a 2.83V input.

Tenses in Equipment Reports: Present tense for anything that’s a persistent characteristic; past tense for anything about the review unit that was measured or perceived.

Example:

“The Goldblatt Synthesis 3 loudspeaker has two drive units, is covered in brown wood-grain vinyl, and costs $25. It suffered from a complete lack of high frequencies, and the bass was boomy.”

The Conclusion, however, should be written in the present tense, as it represents a reviewer’s final and, presumably, immutable verdict:

“On the basis of my listening tests and measurements [all referred to in the body of the report in the past tense], I can assure you that the Goldblatt Synthesis 3 is possibly the worst hi-fi component released by a so-called high-end company.”

STEREOPHILE STYLES

Our “base” style is the Chicago Manual of Style, but rules laid out in this style guide take precedence over Chicago rules.

General

A period or other sentence-ending punctuation mark should be followed by only a single word space. There should be no double, triple, quadruple (etc.) word spaces anywhere in your file.

Paragraphs should not be separated by an extra line space, only a hard return. To ensure this, go to Settings/View and click “All” in under “Show Non-Printing Characters.” This will allow you to see other marks that will not print but may affect the layout—in Word and eventually in InDesign.

All quotations take double quote marks. The only exceptions are quotations within a quotation, which take single quotes. We use “curly” quotes, also called “Smart Quotes” in word and “Typographer’s Quotes” in InDesign. On the other hand, for inches and feet, straight quotes are used. On a Mac, hitting Cmd-Z immediately after typing the quote will turn a curly quote back to straight.

Periods and commas come before (inside) the close-quotes; colons and semicolons come after (outside) the close-quotes. Examples:

Richard Nixon said, “I am not a crook.”

“I don’t think this makes me look fat,” said Piglet.

He once called these types of products “obsolete”: turntables, 8-track tape players, cassette players.

Accents: Be sure to include all accented characters in proper nouns and foreign phrases as they appear in whatever you’re quoting or referring to. These occur even in some words in US English: décor, naïve, café, the prefix über-, etc. On a Mac, these can be typed simply by holding down the letter key until a list of accented characters appears. Then type in the number of the character with the desired accent.

Ellipses: We now use proper ellipsis characters (…) and not a series of three periods separated by spaces. When the first part of a quoted passage is a complete sentence, whether in its original or its quoted form, insert a period and then a space before inserting the ellipsis character and a space after the ellipsis character before starting the next sentence.

The spirit of our American radicalism is destructive and aimless. … On the other side, the conservative party … is timid, and merely defensive of property. … It does not build, nor write, nor cherish the arts, nor foster religion, nor establish schools.

Apostrophes: roman when preceding word is italic; example: Stereophile’s. Also: use apostrophe, not single open quotation mark, to indicate omitted opening letters/syllable in contractions: ’Cause I said so, not ‘Cause I said so.

Commas: For clarity and ease of reading, we use the serial or Oxford comma before the and at the end of a series or list: “I eat mostly wheat chaff, oat hulls, and grits.” An example of the confusion that omission of this comma can cause: “I’d like to thank my parents, Madonna and the Pope.” Also, no comma should be used between phrases unless there’s a new subject. Example: “I am going to the store and then to the theater.” Not: “I am going to the store, and then to the theater.” But: “I am going to the store, and then I am going to the theater.”

Other than subheads, boldface should be rare. It is occasionally used in quotes, where it was used in the original. And it is used in the Specifications section: See separate style sheet.

We insert no wordspaces on either side of a hyphen (-), an en dash (–), or an em dash (—). On a Mac, an en dash (or en-dash) can be typed by holding down the option key while typing a hyphen. An em dash (or em-dash) can be typed by holding down the Option and Shift keys and typing a hyphen.

Use the simplest punctuation that does the job: never an em dash where a comma or semicolon will do, never a semicolon where a period will do. The em dash earns its place only where simpler marks fail: paired dashes setting off an interrupter, an appositive containing commas, a true break in thought. Example: “The unit includes internal storage; a 2TB SSD is standard” (not “…internal storage—a 2TB SSD is standard”).

Joining word pairs: A slash joins named states, settings, and alternatives—the On/Off switch, Standby/On, an A/B test, MM/MC, L/R, 33/45rpm—including multi-position selectors: the LP/CD/AUX/TAPE selector. The slash is kept when the pair modifies a noun (the On/Off switch, not the on-off switch). A hyphen joins oppositional pairs used as compound modifiers: an on-off comparison, a left-right balance adjustment, a push-pull output stage. The en dash is reserved for ranges of numbers (20Hz–20kHz), negative numbers (–3dB), and compound modifiers containing an open element (a Recommended Components–list shopper); do not use an en dash to join word pairs.

“For example” and similar movable connectives (for instance, say, that is): comma placement should match attachment. Use paired commas only when the phrase interrupts: “It is, for example, possible to bypass the DSP.” When it introduces the example that follows, omit the comma after it: “You’ve surely used USB with a computer, for example to connect to external storage.” A trailing gloss takes only the comma before it: “…a different preset for movie watching, for example.”

Capitalize the first word after a colon when what follows is a complete sentence, able to stand on its own: “Power is handled by two linear supplies: One feeds the USB port and the system processor…” Lowercase when what follows is a fragment or list: “Two sources: local files and Qobuz.”

In quotations, respect the words as spoken, but the punctuation is the editor’s: add or adjust punctuation inside a quote (commas, compound-modifier hyphens) whenever house style requires it and the speaker’s meaning is preserved.

Titles of newspaper or magazine articles or essays, book chapters, individual songs, and individual TV episodes get initial caps and are enclosed in double quotes; eg, “MQA, DRM, and Other Four-Letter Words,” Bob Dylan’s “Like a Rolling Stone,” George and Ira Gershwin’s “But Not for Me,” the Bonanza episode “Leprechauns.”

The style for recurring titles of columns and departments is initial caps, roman, no quotes: Letters, As We See It, Analog Corner; only the title of a specific month’s installment would be enclosed in quotes

Not however that position descriptions that are not formal titles are not capped even when they precede the name: “But electronic engineer Jeremy Blyer insists … .”

Check and confirm all proper nouns: spellings of names of people, groups, companies, products, and titles, including abbreviations, initialisms, and acronyms, including any punctuation or incapping. Rules of thumb: If you’re unsure of a spelling or style, look it up. If you’re sure of a spelling or style, look it up.

Names starting with “The”: When the official name of a musical group starts with an article (The National, The Who), the first letter of the article is capped, as it would be in any title. Note, however, that sometimes the “the” is just a “the.” E.g., “We reviewed the Who album in last month’s issue.”

Foreign phrases (musical directions, pianissimo, etc.): italics, unless commonly accepted in English and are in standard dictionaries of US English.

Key signatures: capped (Symphony 6 in D). If a key is minor, the letter should also be capped and followed by “Minor”: (Symphony 9 in D Minor);

Possessives: add s to all proper names ending in s. Examples: Jansons’s musical acumen,” “Wes’s favorite cat.”

Plural possessives: use apostrophes after the second or last name only if what is possessed is a shared entity or quantity, on both or all names if what is possessed is possessed singly by each person: Barack and Michelle’s marriage, Obama’s and Bush’s administrations

size/sized: medium-size item, but CD-sized damping disc; add d when referring to something of specific size (CD), no d if a general size range (medium)

Sounds: italicize words meant to indicate sounds: a hrrrm quality, or a shhhh or sssss sound, or he reproduces the ehs and ihs very well—but don’t italicize onomatopoeic words such as ding-dong or hiss or zing.

Editorial interpolations, stage directions: italics & brackets (coded with braces), as in [laughter], l/c L

References to Recordings in Equipment Reports, Columns, Etc.

Recordings are used as references in the auditioning of audio equipment: An essential part of every equipment report is the reviewer’s reporting on what he or she hears in a specific component’s reproduction of the sound of a specific recording. That way, the interested reader can listen to the same recording through his/her own system and compare his/her impressions with those of the writer. So in addition to precise, vivid, detailed descriptions of the sound, the reader needs all the information necessary to find and obtain a copy of the recording. Examples follow:

Rock and Jazz: artist or group name, album title, and “track title.” On first mention, this should be followed, in parentheses, by the format, label, and catalog number of the recording, and the streaming service, if any. Include catalog numbers whenever they exist—but note that downloads and streams rarely have catalog numbers. Examples:

The RP-600M showed me anew the elegant simplicity and intoxicating virtuosity of Skip James’s piano, guitar, and singing. I’ve rarely experienced the full intricacies of his piano playing as I did when listening to “Little Cow, Little Calf Blues,” from Blues from the Delta (16/44.1 FLAC, Vanguard 79517-2/Qobuz).

“Had to Cry Today,” from Blind Faith’s eponymous studio album (AIFF rip from Japanese CD, Polydor UICY-9168)

Keith Jarrett’s The Köln Concert (24/48 FLAC file, ECM)

Classical: title of work, names of conductor, solo singers (if their singing is specifically discussed) and/or principal instrumentalists (ditto), and/or name of orchestra or chamber or vocal ensemble. Obviously, there are many different ways to engagingly present this information. On first mention, this should be followed, in parentheses, by the format, label, and catalog number of the recording, and the streaming service, if any. Examples:

violinist Marianne Rônez and Affetti Musicali’s recording of Biber’s Mystery Sonatas (CD, Winter & Winter 910 029-2)

Robert Silverman playing Beethoven’s Piano Sonata 30 in E, Op.109 (24/44.1 FLAC, OrpheumMasters/Qobuz)

Liszt’s piano transcription of the Solemn March to the Holy Grail, from Wagner’s Parsifal, as performed by Daniel Barenboim on his On My New Piano (24/96 AIFF download, Deutsche Grammophon)

Equipment Review Format

Titles of articles or reviews should be flush left, with the proper capitalization (but not in all caps). Bylines should appear immediately beneath the title and should consist of your name without the word “By.”

See complete guides to Equipment Report Specifications and Associated Equipment sidebars at the end of this guide.

Plurals and Possessives

Plurals are made without apostrophes: 1980s (not 1980’s), CDs (not CD’s), DVDs (not DVD’s), DVD-As (not DVD-A’s), etc. An apostrophe can be used to indicate a plural only when necessary to avoid confusion or awkwardness. Examples: when making a plural of a single letter or number: “He got three A’s on his report card.” Or when, say, a product’s model name ends with a lower-case letter: “two Vandersteen 2Ce’s” or “LS 3/5a’s.” This makes it clear that the s indicating a plural is not part of the model name.

Form possessives of words ending in s by adding apostrophe and s; eg, “Gladys’s hat,” not “Gladys’ hat.”

Companies are generally considered to be singular collective entities, not plurals: “D’Agostino is [not are] working on several improvements to its [not their] new line of SACD players”; “It [Krell] will be releasing a five-disc changer in September.” However, this can also be varied by referring to groups or individuals within the company, for instance: “Dan D’Agostino is working on an improved version of his SACD player.” Or you can use a noun like “the company” or “the firm”; eg, “All of the company’s [instead of “its”] products are finished in polished aluminum.”

Numbers and Dates

Spell out numbers from one to nine: “the turntable has three speeds,” “it’s a two-channel recording” (but: a 5.1-channel soundtrack), “each Infinity speaker has 16 tweeters.” The sole exception is when followed by a unit of measure, including time. So, four rabbits, but 6 ohms and 7 years.

When specifying two different quantities or sets of numbers next to or near one other, it’s clearer to spell out numbers for one and use digits for the other: “The amp requires twelve 300B tubes.”

No comma in four-digit numbers: $4959, 3000 years, 1500 dealers, etc.

Use commas to delineate thousands, millions, billions, etc., in numbers of five or more digits: $45,000, 120,000, a population of 3,500,000, etc.

A sentence cannot begin with digits; spell out numbers at the beginning of a sentence: “Sixteen tubes are used in this amplifier,” “Nineteen-ninety-five was the first year of production.” Of course, such sentences can always be reworded or reordered: “This amplifier uses 16 tubes.” “The first year of production was 1995.”

Decades and centuries are never spelled out, and century is not capped: “During the 1970s,” “In the 19th century.” When decades are indicated with only the last two numbers, we precede them with an apostrophe, in standard contraction style: the ’70s. Be sure this is an actual apostrophe (’) and not a single open quote (‘). If your word-processing software tries to “help” by inverting this, insert the apostrophe manually: in Windows, use the far-right number keypad with Num Lock on: Alt+0146.

Use numerals for 5.1-channel, 7.1-channel, etc., but spell out two-channel, five-channel, etc. Use a hyphen only when these phrases are used adjectivally: a two-channel recording, but recorded in two channels (not two-channels).

A range of numbers is written with en-dash (–) between two numbers; eg, 20–120Hz. Do not use a hyphen (-) and do not use an em-dash (—). If you begin a range with from, you must complete it with to. Example: from 20 to 60Hz (not: from 20–60Hz).

Impedances are expressed using numerals and “ohms” written out: 4 ohms, not four ohms or 4Ω.

Abbreviations, Symbols, Units of Measurement, Idiomatic Usages

No word spaces between numbers and symbols or abbreviations; eg, 25Hz, 20MHz, 20kHz, 64MB, 92dB, 35W, 40Wpc, 120V, 12oz, 180gm, 20.5kg, 3m, 120mm (note capitalizations). A common exception, for readability: 21 lb (not 21lb or 21 lbs).

Use kilohertz instead of thousands of Hertz; eg, 2kHz, not 2000Hz; 2.5kHz, not 2500Hz; 20Hz–20kHz, not 20–20,000Hz; etc.

A/B comparison (not AB).

A/V or audio/video (not A-V or AV or av or audio-video).

In adjectival constructions, the point of hyphenation is to avoid ambiguity. Hence, connect numbers and units of measurement with a hyphen; eg, 32-bit file, 7-gigabyte FLAC file, 15-pin connector, 16-bit/44.1kHz FLAC file. No hyphen in non-adjectival constructions: a word depth of 16 bits. When an adverb modifies an adjective, no hyphen is used: the wildly popular amplifier. Except that when that adverb is “well,” a hyphen is used: a well-regarded loudspeaker.

Digital Resolutions: bit depth first, followed by sampling rate: 16/44.1, 24/48, 24/96, etc. (not 44.1kHz/16-bit or 44.1/16)

We no longer use IEEE1394; use FireWire (note spelling and capitalization).

We spell RS232 with no hyphen or spaces (not RS-232 or RS 232).

We use AES3, not AES/EBU. S/PDIF is a data format; S/PDIF data can be delivered over electrical connection (RCA or BNC) or optical (typically TosLink).

Rather than writing out “degrees” and “inch/inches” and “foot/feet,” use the degree symbol (°), inch mark ("), and foot mark ('). Foot and inch marks in Stereophile are straight quotes.) If Smart Quotes are turned on in Word, each time you try to type a straight quote (double or single), it will become curly. If you’re trying to type the character for inches or feet, you can undo this on a Mac by immediately typing Cmd-Z.

When such a term begins a sentence, spell out the number and the unit of measurement; eg, “Thirty-two degrees Fahrenheit is the temperature at which water freezes.” Whenever possible, of course, reword to avoid having to spell out numbers: “Water freezes at a temperature of 32°F.”

Omit periods in abbreviations of common Latin words and phrases, and do not italicize them: eg (not e.g. or e.g.), vs (not vs. or vs or versus), ie (not i.e. or i.e.), etc.

The correct rendering for those high-rez video (and sometimes audio) discs is Blu-ray.

The following abbreviations take hyphens: BD-ROM, CD-ROM, CD-R, CD-RW, DVD-A, D-VHS, DTS-ES, D-ILA.

analog, dialog, catalog—no British ue on the end : (not analogue, dialogue, catalogue)

A speaker’s drivers are covered with a grille (not a grill).

set up is a verb, setup is a noun, and set-up is an adjective. Examples: I set up the speakers. What do you think of my speaker setup? That’s a well-set-up system.

email (not e-mail)

website (not web site, Web site, Website)

internet (not Internet). But Ethernet (not ethernet).

preamp (not pre-amp)

midbass to describe a range of frequencies (not mid-bass). mid/bass to describe a single driver that covers the midrange and bass frequencies (not mid-bass). Note that italics here are for emphasis only: These terms should not be italicized.

Los Angeles is abbreviated L.A. (with periods, because “LA” is the postal abbreviation for Louisiana.) The United States and United Kingdom are US (not U.S.) and UK (not U.K.).

Avoid UK spellings: theater, not theatre; fiber, not fibre; center, not centre; etc.

Words and abbreviations (or initialisms) that begin with a vowel sound take the indefinite article an; eg, an HDTV set, an LCD panel.

Spell out “microphone” whenever you can without it seeming ponderous. The abbreviations for microphone are mike, miked, miking (not mic, miced, micing). There’s no good answer for this one. We made a call.

Carefully check spellings of manufacturer names and component model names. Be especially careful to use correct capitalization, hyphens, and spaces (or any lacks thereof).

The publication of the technical specifications for the Compact Disc format is spelled “Red Book” (two words, both capped).

Note spelling and capitalization in Blu-ray. The r is not capitalized, nor does Blu take an e. Abbreviated BD.

The names of common units of measurement named after people are lower-cased when spelled out, capped when abbreviated: watt/W, volt/V, ampere/A, ohm, farad/F, etc. When the unit is a symbol or symbols, there’s no space (10V, 12kHz). When it is a word, use a space: 8 ohms.

RECORD REVIEWS: HEAD MATTER

There are three basic styles for record-review headings: Classical, Jazz, and everything else. Each is discussed separately below. Note that individual musicians are listed only for Classical and Jazz recordings, and not for recordings of Rock, Pop, Folk, World, etc. Basically, each heading comprises six or seven fields, the fields separated by empty line spaces below:

Classical Head Matter

or, when more than one composer is represented on the recording (a recital, a concerto pairing, any multi-composer collection): all the musicians, in standard musicians-line form, main performer first — in a lieder recital, the singer, then the pianist; with an orchestra, the soloist, then orchestra/conductor. A named ensemble is given by its name (Trio Boccherini); an unnamed ad hoc group is given as its members (name, instrument; name, instrument; …). No separate musicians line follows — each performer is named exactly once. (This deliberately avoids star billing.) In short: one composer, composer-led; more than one, performer-led.
